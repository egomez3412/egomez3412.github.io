Raiser Senior Services—The Stratford (A)


Jennifer Raiser, president of Raiser Senior Services (RSS), walked through the lobby of The Stratford in April 2002 and admired the beautiful flowers arranged throughout the room. The lobby projected the image of a fancy hotel—which is just what her father had wanted when he personally managed construction of the high-rise building in San Mateo, California. The marble fireplace complemented the wood paneling and textured walls, and everything shone in the warm light provided by the abundant ceiling lighting. They had put in three times more light fixtures than the number found in the typical building lobby—after all, people in their 80s and 90s simply need more light.

Raiser looked around and wondered when they would need to replace some of the furniture that had welcomed residents and their guests since The Stratford opened in 1992. It was important that the building retain its upscale look, but she had to be careful with expenses. The Stratford was one of the most prestigious—and most expensive—continuing care retirement communities (CCRCs) in California, but management struggled to keep the operation from losing money each year.

The Stratford was designed to cater to the needs of the high-income aging population in the Bay Area. The 12-story building’s 64 units housed 90 residents with an average age of 86. People generally moved into the Stratford while they could still live independently, and the lifetime guarantee of ongoing health care and various services such as meals, housecleaning, chauffeur services, exercise classes, and planned activities provided a strong incentive to join the community. The Stratford promised continuing care to its residents through the various phases of aging—from independent living, to assisted living, to skilled nursing.1 Elderly local men and women sold their homes and bought condominiums at The Stratford with the plan of staying until they passed away. For the most part, the residents who selected The Stratford could afford any type of ongoing care they wanted—and they chose to live in The Stratford because of its reputation for providing the best senior services, dining, and health care in the area.

    Background and Family History

    Raiser grew up in San Mateo, California, the middle of three children. Her father, John Raiser, moved to the United States from Greece when he was 16 and met his wife Helen in California. From the time they were born, Jennifer and her siblings Phillip and Cynthia helped their father with the family business of designing, building, and operating real estate in the Bay Area. When Jennifer was not sweeping the floors or running errands, she was answering phones and taking messages. As she recalls:

    I grew up with the business. My father arrived in the United States with $200 in his pocket and built everything on handshakes and long-term relationships. He started with a construction company, which evolved into real estate development, which evolved into building and owning commercial properties here in the San Francisco Bay Area. He always acted with integrity and was extremely quality oriented. He believed that buildings should be built to last 100 years.

    Jennifer went to high school in San Mateo and attended college at Harvard University, where she studied English and American literature. After graduation, she took a marketing job with Procter & Gamble. Four years later she returned to Boston, this time to get her MBA from Harvard Business School. By the time she graduated from business school in 1990, the family business consisted of 18 properties and was being run by her father John, with assistance from her younger brother Phillip. (Their older sister, an organic farmer, was an owner but did not participate in the management of the business.)

    Immediately after business school, Jennifer took a job with a consulting firm. However, after a year of traveling five days a week, she decided she would be happier with a job that required less travel. As Jennifer was thinking about a new job, her father was in the process of expanding the family business. The idea of starting a senior services community was beginning to take shape.


    Raiser Senior Services

    Throughout the late 1980s, as John Raiser watched his mother age, he thought about the types of buildings that would best suit an older population. He looked around and concluded that the Bay Area lacked any senior living residences that he would consider moving to one day. As a result, he began planning a new development designed around the needs of seniors. Jennifer described her father’s thought process:

    My father spent a great deal of time thinking about how to break the mold of the usual kind of nursing home or assisted-living program. Instead of the typical hospital-like building, he envisioned a luxury hotel with services geared to seniors. This was both a philosophical decision and an economic one, as he knew he could get the most money per square foot from a condominium that offered all these additional services.

    When John found a great piece of land in 1990 that was across the street from a 16-acre park in San Mateo, he focused on making his vision a reality in the form of a new building called The Stratford.

    With the help of a few consultants who understood the needs of seniors, construction started in 1991. The Raiser family realized it was entering a complicated industry requiring different skills than those needed for the other parts of the Raiser real estate portfolio. This realization coincided with Jennifer’s decision to leave consulting, so she and her husband moved to California to help out. She explained: “The idea of doing something in operations was very appealing, and the new business seemed innovative. I also liked the service management aspect and felt that the world was moving towards a service orientation. I hadn’t seen a retirement community that I really respected and thought I could make an impact. Of course, I was also extremely naïve.”

    In late 1991 Jennifer took over the operations of The Stratford with the goal of welcoming its first residents in August 1992. During that time, Jennifer learned about the industry, developed policies, created manuals, and put together a service plan to meet the regulations governing CCRCs. Jennifer knew that her family’s reputation was on the line, so The Stratford had to provide outstanding

For the first couple of years I was working 16 hours a day and was on call every moment.” By 1994 nearly 90% of the condominiums had been sold to independent elderly people in the area—44% of whom were still there in April 2002. Five years after Jennifer moved to California, her father unexpectedly passed away.

    The Raiser Organization acquired two additional CCRCs in the Bay Area. In 1996 the family bought a failing 120-unit CCRC in San Francisco that had been run by a nonprofit. After minor renovations, Raiser reopened this facility as The Carlisle. Two years later, the Raiser Organization purchased another distressed CCRC—this time a smaller facility in downtown Palo Alto—and in 2001 it completed renovations and reopened the facility as Webster House. As of April 2002 both these facilities—as well as The Stratford—were owned and operated by RSS..

    As RSS grew from one property to three, Jennifer knew she needed help managing the business. Although her brother Phillip had pitched in over the years, he was busy with the construction company and commercial properties. Her mother lived at The Stratford and supervised many aesthetic elements, including arranging the flowers each week, but Jennifer needed full-time assistance with operations. In 1998 she recruited Richard Ashton, the executive director of an upscale retirement community in Southern California, to join her as the new chief operating officer of all three properties. Together they managed the business and focused on building a team for each of the three properties. (See Exhibit 1 for an organizational chart and Exhibit 2 for management bios.)

    Senior Housing Options

    According to the American Association of Homes and Services for the Aging (AAHSA), most older people preferred to live in their own homes as long as possible. However, as people aged, they often needed additional services. Some older people received help from family members, while others relied on community services such as adult day care, transportation, chore services, personal care and home health care. When The Stratford opened in 1992, a number of options were emerging to serve in various types of facilities such as senior housing, nursing homes, assisted living, and CCRCs. Senior housing consisted of complexes designed to accommodate the older population and helped make community services accessible to residents. Nursing homes (sometimes referred to as “skilled-nursing” establishments) were tailored to people who needed 24-hour care. Assisted-living establishments were a relatively new idea in the early 1990s and were created to bridge the gap between independent living and nursing home care. This type of environment was best suited to older people who needed help with certain daily activities such as getting dressed, taking medications, and doing housekeeping and laundry but who did not necessarily need supervision throughout the day.

    CCRCs such as The Stratford covered the entire spectrum. They offered people a long-term contract that provided services and nursing care, usually in one location. According to AAHSA, “Most people move into CCRCs when they are active and healthy because of the wide range of amenities and programs. People who live in CCRCs also have the assurance of knowing that as they age, most of their long-term health care needs will be met without the need to relocate outside of the community.”4 Some types of CCRCs (such as The Stratford) required residents to buy condominiums, while others offered rental properties coupled with or without an entrance fee. All types required some form of monthly fee. Some properties were run by charitable organizations as nonprofits, while others, such as The Stratford, were designed to be profitable.

    Competition

    The CCRC industry was started by churches and charitable organizations, which continued to dominate the entrance fee-based portion of the market. Residents moved in and left their unit to the church or a charity, which would then remarket it to new potential residents. The charitable facilities had an advantage in attracting residents due to their perceived trustworthiness, but these organizations suffered from a lack of business management skills.

    The for-profit segment of the business was started as an adjunct to the nursing home industry and had only recently begun to develop. A number of public companies, including Aegis Corporation, Sunrise Housing Corporation, and LifeCare Services, were enjoying their status as Wall Street’s darlings in the early 1990s but started faltering in the later 1990s as the industry became saturated.

    During the mid-1990s Raiser noted that several larger hospitality companies were entering the senior care industry. For example, by 2001 Marriott was operating 156 senior-oriented properties in the United States.5 (See Exhibit 4 for operating statistics and financials for Marriott’s Senior Living Services division.) In 1987 Hyatt started a division called Classic Residence by Hyatt that was operating 15 high-end properties by 2002. Hyatt was in the process of building a new 338-unit luxury CCRC in Palo Alto. Scheduled to open in 2005, this property was about 10 miles south of The Stratford, on the campus of Stanford University. Services and fees were expected to compare with those of The Stratford, and it was likely that the two properties would compete for new residents. A couple of the Hyatt architects had taken a tour of The Stratford and commented that the new Palo Alto facility was going to be one of their most upscale properties—in fact, they planned to market it as “a building like The Stratford.” (See Exhibit 5 for a fact sheet about the proposed Hyatt facility in Palo Alto.)

    According to Raiser’s research into the senior care industry, elderly people tended to move into senior communities within 10 to 15 miles of where they had been living. As a result, competition tended to be quite local. In the San Mateo area, The Stratford competed with a nearby facility called The Peninsula Regent that had more units, was less expensive, and was not considered as luxurious. Stratford management believed that its target customer was not selecting based on price. Instead, its residents opted for The Stratford because they knew friends who had moved there and they liked the services and reputation of the building.

    Although there were other senior facilities in the area, Raiser believed that the biggest competitor was the status quo. Rather than selecting among various properties, most people in their target market decided between moving to The Stratford and staying at home with private nursing aides who cost about $20 to $25 an hour. Many people simply did not want to move so late in life and opted to stay in their homes as long as possible.

    Regulatory Environment

    All CCRCs were highly regulated by the state and required various forms of accreditation. In California, the California Department of Social Services oversaw all aspects of running a CCRC. In addition to monitoring the financials, they identified the proper disclosures required, fielded resident complaints, and ensured that seniors’ interests were represented in the legislature. The Stratford was also required to comply with various regulations established by the California Department of Real Estate.     Overview of The Stratford

    The Stratford was located on one acre adjacent to Central Park in San Mateo. Of the 64 units, 37 overlooked the park, and these sold at a substantial premium compared with the others. Overall, the building contained 3 one-bedroom, 49 two-bedroom, 9 three-bedroom, and 3 four-bedroom units. Purchase prices had ranged from $550,000 to $2.4 million the previous year. (See Exhibit 6 for a list of all units that had changed hands since The Stratford opened.)

    Residents and Facility

    In the spring of 2002, 70% of the residents were female, and the residents’ ages ranged from 67 to

    102. There were 90 residents in total, approximately 50 of whom were entirely independent. The other 40 required a range of assisted-living services, with a few residents requiring enough assistance that it made sense for them to live full time in the assisted-living wing of the property. (This wing— called the Laurel Wing—contained nine private suites and always had a nurse onsite.) To conveniently serve its residents, The Stratford was licensed to provide assisted-living care throughout the property.

    In addition to the residential units, the building contained a number of common areas, including a lobby, dining room, spa, indoor swimming pool, greenhouse, salon, movie theater, party space, and library. (See Exhibit 7 for a complete list of amenities at The Stratford.) The building was also one block from a downtown area with many shops and restaurants. Residents frequently left their units to enjoy the common areas and nearby activity centers.

    Staff

    The Stratford was managed by a team of people with extensive experience in senior care. In addition to Jennifer Raiser and Ashton, the management team included Trudy Ferree, the director of health services, and Steve Simmons, the executive chef. Jennifer’s brother, Phillip, also helped as needed, although he worked full time managing the family’s other 18 residential and commercial properties. Other key personnel included the concierge, the director of maintenance, the director of housekeeping, and the maitre d’. Overall, The Stratford had 79 full-time and part-time people working at the facility ranging from senior managers to on-call dog walkers. Raiser believed that The Stratford’s staff was competent, hardworking, and committed to high-quality care and service. In fact, Raiser often received letters praising its performance. (See Exhibit 8 for sample letters from residents.)

    Technology

    The team used rudimentary technology such as walkie-talkies, cell phones, and pagers to stay in touch during the day and maintained some resident information on computers. Other forms of technology at the facility included motion detectors in the units, security cameras, and pull cords throughout the building—all considered state of the art when The Stratford was constructed in 1991. The pull cords, located in all bedrooms, bathrooms, and kitchens, were used to alert the front desk and the nurses in case of an emergency. According to Raiser, “Those pull cords have saved the lives of at least four of our residents.” With respect to other uses of technology, she explained:

    Business Model

    The Stratford was structured so that the Raiser Organization would make money in three areas: management fees (paid out of the monthly fees, less operating costs, and targeted to be 5% of the operating budget), real estate transaction fees (7% on each sale paid by the seller), and the appreciation of the real estate. Each time a unit was sold, 3% went to an operating reserve for capital improvements as well as for other funding needs not covered by monthly fees. After the 3% assessment, RSS received 75% of any price appreciation on the unit, with the other 25% going to the estate of the person who left.

    During the planning stages in the early 1990s, the Raiser Organization projected substantial profits from the appreciation of the units, both through inflation and higher real estate prices. By April 2002, however, it was becoming increasingly apparent that the profits from this area were not going to be what management had expected. Inflation in the United States had been hovering at an all-time low, and condominiums (particularly in the senior care industry) did not share in the general real estate appreciation that had been occurring in the Bay Area. Furthermore, the units were not turning over as frequently as management had expected. Occupants at The Stratford were living longer than the national average, in part due to the convenient health care and other services provided in the building. As Ashton explained, “Because we provide assistance and do all of the household tasks such as changing lightbulbs, we’ve actually removed some of the accident-risk factors. And I think we have a good wellness program—we’re very successful at keeping people in this community healthy.” As a result of the lower-than-expected income from real estate transactions and appreciation fees, RSS was increasingly focused on ensuring that the business could profit operationally.


    The Stratford—Operations and Costs

    Since its opening in 1992, The Stratford had lost money or barely broken even each year. The numbers for 2000 and 2001 were among the worst to date, requiring a special assessment on the residents and a withdrawal from the reserve fund to compensate for operating losses. (See Exhibit 9 for The Stratford’s income statement for 2000-2001.) Management believed that the primary reason for the operating shortfall was that resident monthly fees were never at levels commensurate with the ever-increasing labor costs. Each year fees were raised an average of 4% to cover cost increases; however, the shortfall remained. According to Raiser, “Residents were strongly opposed to any increases. They expected us to work harder or discontinue services as an alternative.”

    At the end of 2001, management decided to implement a more substantial fee increase of 20% to raise the fees to a level that would allow for break-even operations. (See Exhibit 10 for a history of The Stratford’s monthly fees.) Residents begrudgingly paid the new fees of $4,150 per month for

From the time that Ashton had joined RSS, he had worked on establishing more operating controls and methods for overseeing expenses. At the beginning, he found it difficult to clearly understand how things worked and exactly what was being spent in various areas. He elaborated:

    The Stratford is known throughout the industry as a sophisticated, high-end product. After spending some time at The Stratford I came to realize that one of the reasons for the high level of service was the sheer determination of the Raisers. I would look around and see Jennifer serving in the dining room and her mother, Helen, tidying up the property and creating all the flower arrangements in the building. Although there were standards, the Raisers’ approach meant that activities were not always articulated in formal policies and procedures. It was somewhat unsettling.

At first I thought we could just make changes by superimposing a bunch of systems and building a stronger infrastructure, but it wasn’t so easy. I learned that I needed to respect the current systems and the company culture in order to effect change. Initially I focused on streamlining aspects of our accounting and establishing better methods of monitoring our expenses. And although there were several staff positions that needed to be eliminated, the residents were so attached to some of the individuals, we had to move with care.

    Soon after Ashton started he helped recruit additional management in the two areas with the highest expenses—health services and dining. Although this was an additional expense, Ashton and Raiser hoped that the new managers would ultimately add more rigor to the operations.

    Health Services

    About 25% of The Stratford’s expenses related to providing health care to its residents. The Stratford’s health care services had been run by Trudy Ferree since September 2000. She had a staff of four licensed vocational nurses (LVNs), who were paid about $22 per hour, and six certified nursing assistants (CNAs), who were paid about $12 per hour.7 Ferree ensured that there was always an LVN onsite by having three shifts (7a.m.-3p.m., 3p.m.-11p.m., 11p.m.-7a.m.), and she kept a CNA onsite every day during the day shift. The health services team had a number of responsibilities, including assessing each person upon his or her moving into The Stratford, staying abreast of any health-related changes, assisting (for example, giving out medication) about 20% of the residents, responding to those who needed more acute care, caring for patients living in the Laurel Wing, and staying in touch with residents’ families to share information. According to Raiser, CCRC regulations did not require having an LVN onsite around the clock, but it helped with marketing and with risk management. As she described, “Our staffing is much, much higher than what is required, but we feel that this is an advantage in the marketplace because people don’t move here because they

    As our residents age it is almost inevitable that we will need more staffing. It will be more taxing—and not just physically. It took so much time. And it’s intense too. She had to trust me when I told her that her beloved husband was no longer functional. And he used to be in a big position at a Fortune 500 company. It was very hard to accept. As a person ages it can get very complex. You’re dealing with family, and you’re dealing with emotions. Are we ready for this?

    Dining Services

    The second-largest expense was dining services. Under the management of executive chef Simmons, The Stratford offered dinner every night to residents and their guests. Dinner was an important part of daily life, as it was one of the primary group activities at the building. The Stratford prided itself on its cuisine—Simmons had previously been at four-star restaurants in San Francisco such as Campton Place and the Lark Creek Inn. As Raiser explained, “Food is such an important element of senior communities—if it is not good, people complain all the time. And for the healthier people here, we only see them at dinner, so the gourmet meals are viewed as a key product of the expensive monthly fees.”

    Each evening, Simmons had a staff of three cooks, one dedicated pastry chef, one dishwasher, and one utility person to help in the kitchen. On any given night, about 40 residents ate in the dining room and another 25 to 35 residents requested that dinners be brought to their rooms between 5:30

    p.m. and 7 p.m. (Residents called the front desk before 4 p.m. if they wanted room service for that evening. The front desk allowed a maximum of seven requests for each 15-minute time slot.) Simmons published a menu every two weeks with entrée selections such as pan roasted rack of lamb with mint gremolata, grilled day boat scallops with saffron glaze, and pan sautéed duck breast with sour cherry demi glace. (See Exhibit 13 for a sample menu.) Each night residents were given a choice among two featured selections plus a consistent set of items always available, such as chicken or pasta dishes. Residents were asked for their preferences in advance, but they had the option to change their choice when they ordered. On nights featuring particularly good meals and on weekends, residents often brought guests. Guests were charged a nominal fee of $20 each, and sometimes there were as many as 20. According to Simmons: “We don’t really want to increase the fee because my food costs—at about $6 per person per dinner—can be covered with that price.”

    Dinner started at 5:30, and most people ate between 5:45 and 7:15. According to Simmons, “People do not linger—most stay only 45 minutes. The soup goes out, and two minutes later they are looking for their entrees. But they still want a sophisticated presentation.” Rather than having regular seatings, the guests were allowed to arrive at any time. The dinner guests were served by the maitre d’, his assistant, and a staff of seven waiters, all of whom reported to the maitre d’. On most nights, three to four waiters worked in the dining room, and one to two waiters handled room service.8 Waiters were paid $12 an hour and did not receive any tips; most were using the income to supplement a full-time day job. As Ashton said:

    We make a concentrated group effort to produce a certain amount of food in a short period of time. And we also have the room service situation. In a normal retirement community, you would offer room service for an extra charge, but only for people who are truly ill. At The Stratford, it’s available to anyone at any time. Even during the day.     Other Expense Categories

    After health services and dining, the next-largest expense categories were the general and administrative (G&A) costs of running the facility, housekeeping, and resident services. The G&A costs included a 40% allocation to certain RSS staff (Ashton, human resources, and administration) and various smaller items. The category should have included the salary for an executive director, but as of early 2002 Raiser and Ashton had not found anyone with both hotel and health care skills to fill that position. Raiser was acting executive director for the property without taking a salary for that role. The housekeeping category primarily included the expense of the housekeeping staff, which offered standard weekly service to the residents and other services as needed. The resident services category consisted of expenses for the 24-hour front desk staff, a full-time concierge, a driver for the residents, and a part-time dog walker. Raiser believed that these services were not only necessary for operations but also important for marketing purposes in terms of attracting new residents. Raiser commented, “With the new Hyatt competition, services were going to be more important than ever.”
